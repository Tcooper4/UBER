The "issue" — 24 missing hours, not material
Expected 14,640 unique hours, got 14,616. That's 24 missing hours over 20 months = 0.16%.
This is almost certainly the daylight saving time transition. NYC observes DST, and "spring forward" loses an hour each spring. With 2 spring transitions in your window (March 2024 + March 2025), that's 2 hours. The remaining 22 hours are likely zone-hours where literally no Uber/Lyft trips occurred (deep middle-of-night, sparse outer-borough zones), so they don't appear in the TLC aggregation.
Either explanation is fine. Not material.
Finding I want to flag — your DiD signal is huge
Look at Check 4:
Uber pre-CP, in CRZ:   $5.90/mile  (135M trips outside CRZ at $4.68/mi)
Uber post-CP, in CRZ:  $5.95/mile  (CRZ already premium)

The DiD coefficient before any controls:

(5.95 - 5.90) − (4.78 - 4.68) = $0.05 − $0.10 = −$0.05/mile

CRZ fare-per-mile rose less than non-CRZ fare-per-mile after congestion pricing. That's weakly negative, possibly null. The naive descriptive signal is ambiguous.
But this is what makes the analysis interesting:

Volume side: CRZ trips dropped from 45M → 26M (-42%). That's a huge volume effect.
Fare side: roughly flat.

Implication: Congestion pricing reduced demand in the CRZ but didn't push fare-per-mile up much (supply matched the volume drop). That's actually a coherent story — riders cut trips, drivers redirected outside CRZ, equilibrium price held.
Your headline DiD finding may not be "fare-per-mile rose" but rather "volume crashed, fare held." Different story than expected. This is a potential reframing moment — and exactly the kind of thing the case rewards finding.
